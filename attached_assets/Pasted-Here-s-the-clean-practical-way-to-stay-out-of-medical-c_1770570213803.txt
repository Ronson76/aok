Here’s the clean, practical way to stay out of medical-claim, negligence, and “duty of care” traps — with exact things to implement, not fluff.

First: the core principle (burn this in)

AOK must never:

Diagnose

Treat

Monitor health for safety

Promise outcomes

Replace professional judgment

You are providing tools, not care.

Everything you implement should reinforce that separation.

1. Hard boundary: language you MUST use (and avoid)
Allowed language

Use this everywhere (UI, T&Cs, onboarding, toggles):

“Wellbeing”

“Fitness activity”

“Self-tracked”

“Informational only”

“User-initiated”

“Not monitored”

“Does not provide medical advice”

Banned language

Do not use:

“Health monitoring”

“Safety monitoring”

“Early warning”

“Detects issues”

“Ensures safety”

“Keeps you safe”

“Alerts when something is wrong”

Why:
The moment you imply monitoring or detection, you inherit duty of care.

2. Mandatory onboarding consent (non-skippable)

Before fitness or emergency features activate, force this step.

Screen: “Important Notice”

Checkboxes must be separate (not one big “I agree”).

Toggle 1 — Fitness data disclaimer

☐ I understand that fitness and activity features are for personal wellbeing only and do not provide medical advice, diagnosis, or health monitoring.

Toggle 2 — No reliance clause

☐ I understand AOK does not monitor my activity or wellbeing and I must not rely on it for my personal safety or health.

Toggle 3 — Emergency limitation

☐ I understand emergency features do not replace emergency services and may fail due to connectivity, device, or technical issues.

User cannot proceed without all three.

Log:

timestamp

app version

IP/device hash

This matters later.

3. Feature-level toggles (this is critical)

Do not lump permissions together.

A. Fitness tracking toggle

Default: OFF

“Enable fitness activity tracking”

Below it, plain text:

“This records your movement for personal wellbeing insights only. AOK does not monitor, analyse, or act on this data for health or safety purposes.”

B. Social / sharing toggle

“Share fitness activities with others”

Clarify:

“Shared activities are visible only to people you choose. AOK does not supervise or review shared activity.”

C. Live activity / emergency link toggle

This one is key.

“Link fitness activities to emergency features”

With warning text:

“Emergency escalation is manual and user-triggered. AOK does not automatically detect danger, injury, or health events.”

4. Emergency recording (camera + mic) safeguards

This is powerful but risky if framed wrong.

Required UI behaviour

Visible indicator when recording (icon + text)

On activation message:

“Recording has started to document the situation. This is not monitored in real time.”

Required consent (one-time, logged)

☐ I consent to audio/video recording during emergencies for documentation purposes only.

Storage disclaimer (must be explicit)

“Recordings are stored securely and accessed only by authorised parties according to your organisation’s policies.”

Never say:

“for your protection”

“to ensure safety”
Say:

“for documentation”

That distinction matters legally.

5. No automatic interpretation. Ever.

This is where apps get sued.

You must not:

Flag abnormal behaviour

Detect inactivity as a “problem”

Trigger alerts based on pace, HR, stopping, falling, etc.

Suggest injury, distress, or health risk

You can:

Allow the user to press SOS

Allow the user to share live activity

Allow manual escalation only

If you later add automation, it must be:

“User-configured reminder prompts”
Not detection.

6. Kill the “false reassurance” problem

This is subtle but important.

Always show (somewhere visible):

“AOK may not function in all circumstances. Network coverage, device settings, battery life, or software issues may prevent features from working.”

This protects you from:

“Why didn’t the app save them?”

“We relied on it.”

7. Positioning for students & organisations
Students

“Personal wellbeing and safety tools”

“Optional fitness motivation”

“User-controlled sharing”

Organisations (YMCA, universities)

Your wording:

“AOK supports safeguarding procedures but does not replace supervision, policies, or emergency response frameworks.”

That one sentence saves you a world of pain.

8. Absolute red line (do NOT cross)

If you ever:

analyse health metrics

score risk

flag concern

notify someone without user action

You move into:

medical device territory (UK MHRA)

professional negligence

insurance requirements

Right now, you are clean if you keep everything:

optional

user-triggered

informational
